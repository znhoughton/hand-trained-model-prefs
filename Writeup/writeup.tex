\begin{abstract}
A defining property of human language is the ability to trade off
between stored knowledge and abstract knowledge, yet what drives this
trade-off remains poorly understood. In the present study, we
investigate whether transformer language models trained on human-scale
data exhibit a comparable trade-off, using binomial ordering preferences
as a test case. We train six autoregressive language models from scratch
(125M, 350M, and 1.3B parameters) on two corpora (BabyLM and a subset of
C4) and checkpoint models throughout training to examine how ordering
preferences emerge. We find that although all models' preferences are
driven by their experience, smaller models show evidence of learned
abstract preferences, while larger models show little such evidence at
the final checkpoint; however trajectory analyses suggest they are
sensitive to abstract preferences earlier in training. The present study
demonstrates that model size and training data both play a role in the
emergence of abstract knowledge in language models and further
contributes to open-access research by making all models and checkpoints
publicly available.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}\label{introduction}

The ability to trade off between stored and abstract knowledge is
central to human language. A proficient language user must be able to
apply abstract rules productively (for example, generating the plural of
a novel word like \emph{wug} as \emph{wugs}) while simultaneously
drawing on stored knowledge that resists those rules (knowing that the
plural of \emph{goose} is \emph{geese}, not \emph{gooses}). There is
substantial evidence that humans navigate this trade-off dynamically as
a function of linguistic experience
\citep[e.g.,][]{morgan2016abstractknowledgedirect, morgan2024productiveknowledgeitemspecific, houghton2025multiwordrepresentationsminds},
yet the mechanisms driving it remain poorly understood.

In the present study, we investigate whether transformer language models
trained on human-scale data exhibit a comparable trade-off between
stored and abstract knowledge, and whether they mirror what is observed
in humans. Our main contributions are as follows:

\begin{itemize}
\tightlist
\item
  We train six autoregressive language models from scratch on two
  corpora
  \citetext{\citealp[BabyLM,][]{charpentier2025babylmturns3}; \citealp[and
  a subset of C4,][]{raffelExploringLimitsTransfer2020}} at three model
  sizes (125M, 350M, and 1.3B parameters), allowing us to examine how
  training data and scale jointly shape ordering preferences. In order
  to promote open access research, we also make these 6 models (along
  with all checkpoints) publicly accessible.
\item
  We replicate and extend
  \citet{houghton2025roleabstractrepresentations} by examining whether
  models trained on a developmentally plausible amount of data show
  evidence of abstract preferences, rather than solely memorization.
\item
  We examine the full learning trajectory of binomial ordering
  preferences across training allowing us to assess not only whether
  human-like preferences emerge, but when and how they develop.
\item
  We introduce a preference deviation measure that captures the
  deviation between a model's ordering preference and what training
  statistics alone would predict, enabling a more direct test of whether
  model preferences are driven by factors beyond memorization.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Abstract Knowledge and Stored Knowledge in
Humans}\label{abstract-knowledge-and-stored-knowledge-in-humans}

There is strong evidence for both abstract and stored knowledge in
humans, with abstract knowledge being considerably less controversial
\citep[cf.][]{ambridgeStoredAbstractionsRadical2020}. For example, in a
seminal paper, \citet{berkoChildsLearningEnglish1958} demonstrated that
children reliably apply morphological rules to novel words they have
never encountered, yielding the now-famous example of a creature called
a \emph{wug}.

Evidence for abstract knowledge spans virtually every level of
linguistic representation, from syntactic structures \citep[evidenced by
syntactic priming,][]{bock1986syntacticpersistencelanguage}, to
phonological representations \citep[evidenced by phonological
priming,][]{goldinger1992formbasedprimingspoken}, to semantic
representations \citep{meyer1971facilitationrecognizingpairs}. Priming
effects provide compelling evidence for abstraction: for one context to
facilitate processing in another, the two must share some underlying
representation. This elegantly accounts for why, for example, sentences
with entirely different words but a shared syntactic structure can prime
one another.

Abstract knowledge also shapes the learning and processing of specific
constructions. For example, \citet{benorChickenEggProbabilistic2006}
demonstrated that binomial orderings in corpus data (e.g., \emph{bishops
and seamstresses} vs.~\emph{seamstresses and bishops}) are driven by a
range of phonological, semantic, and metrical constraints, such as a
preference to place the more powerful or shorter word first.
\citet{morgan2016abstractknowledgedirect} operationalized these
constraints by coding a set of binomials for these features and fitting
a logistic regression model to corpus data, yielding a single fitted
value per binomial (abstract preference) that summarizes the degree to
which abstract constraints favor one ordering over the other. They found
that abstract preferences predicted human ordering preferences for
low-frequency binomials, while relative frequency (the proportion that a
binomial appears in corpus data in alphabetical ordering relative to
nonalphabetical ordering) predicted the ordering of high-frequency
items. A follow-up study
\citep{morgan2024productiveknowledgeitemspecific} subsequently showed
that while relative frequency remains a strong predictor for
high-frequency binomials, abstract preferences exert an influence across
the full frequency spectrum. Together, these results suggest that humans
draw on stored knowledge for high-frequency binomials while relying on
abstract preferences for lower-frequency items.

The trade-off between abstract and stored knowledge as a function of
language usage has a long history in the literature
\citep[e.g.,][]{bybee1999usagebasedphonology, kapatsinski2010frequencyuseleads, kapatsinskiFrequencyEmergencePrefabs2009, stembergerAreInflectedForms2004, stembergerFrequencyLexicalStorage1986, houghton2025multiwordrepresentationsminds, morgan2016abstractknowledgedirect, morganFrequencydependentRegularizationIterated2016a, morganModelingIdiosyncraticPreferences2015}.
For example, \citet{stembergerAreInflectedForms2004} demonstrated that
inflection errors are less common for high-frequency words than
low-frequency words. Similarly, \citet{bybee1999usagebasedphonology}
showed that \emph{don't} is more phonetically reduced in high-frequency
phrases (e.g., \emph{I don't know}) than in low-frequency ones (e.g.,
\emph{I don't go}). Processing measures have provided convergent
evidence: recognition of constituent words is harder in high-frequency
\citep{kapatsinskiFrequencyEmergencePrefabs2009} and high-predictability
\citep{houghton2025multiwordrepresentationsminds} phrases (e.g.,
recognizing \emph{up} in \emph{pick up}), consistent with holistic
storage of these sequences. Despite this broad empirical support,
however, the mechanism driving the trade-off remains unclear.

\subsection{Accounts of Abstract Knowledge and Stored
Knowledge}\label{accounts-of-abstract-knowledge-and-stored-knowledge}

One influential mechanistic account appeals to neural network models,
which offer an explanation for how abstraction and storage can emerge
from the same underlying system: by learning to map inputs onto
distributional representations that reflect both the statistical
regularities shared across items and the idiosyncratic properties of
individual items, these systems can in principle capture both rule-like
generalizations and item-specific stored knowledge within a single
architecture. Neural networks enable this ability to capture both types
of knowledge through hidden layers, which project the input into a new
representational space where previously intractable classification
boundaries become linearly separable (see Appendix Section~\ref{sec-xor}
for a concrete illustration).

Multi-layer neural networks underlie a great deal of success behind the
transformer architecture \citep{vaswaniAttentionAllYou2017}, which is
the architecture behind nearly every successful language model that has
emerged within the last few years. However, it is unclear whether
transformer models trade off between stored and abstract knowledge in a
way that is similar to humans. Indeed, the evidence for abstract
knowledge altogether is quite mixed
\citep{haleyThisBERTNow2020, lasriSubjectVerbAgreement2022, liAssessingCapacityTransformer2023, li2021areneuralnetworks, houghton2025roleabstractrepresentations, mccoy2023howmuchlanguage, misraLanguageModelsLearn2024, yaoBothDirectIndirect2025, kapatsinski2026transformersperformadaptive, jian2026humans}.

On one hand, there is evidence that GPT-2
\citep{radfordLanguageModelsAre2019} can generate both novel words and
novel syntactic structures \citep{mccoy2023howmuchlanguage}, and that
models can generalize learned knowledge beyond the specific
constructions in which they encounter it
\citep{misraLanguageModelsLearn2024}. For example,
\citet{misraLanguageModelsLearn2024} examined whether models can learn
the rare Article Adjective Numeral Noun construction (e.g., \emph{a
beautiful five days}) without direct exposure, and found that models can
learn it by generalizing from similar structures they have encountered,
such as \emph{a few days}. Similarly,
\citet{kapatsinski2026transformersperformadaptive} demonstrated that
GPT-2 exhibits adaptive partial pooling during training, such that
predictions become increasingly context-specific for frequent contexts
while continuing to draw on broader distributional information for rare
ones, a pattern that closely mirrors the frequency-dependent trade-off
between stored and abstract knowledge observed in humans. Relatedly,
\citet{jian2026humans} examined whether GPT-2's learning trajectory over
the course of training is better characterized by abstraction-first or
exemplar-first accounts. Using a novel divergence-based metric, they
tracked how next-word prediction distributions change across training
checkpoints. Under an exemplar-first account, next-word predictions for
individual items should initially diverge from others in the same class
before converging as training progresses. Instead, they found that
class-level distinctions arise as soon as any item-specific change is
detectable, suggesting that transformer models have an inductive bias
toward abstraction when learning language.

On the other hand, there is evidence that these models rely heavily on
memorization
\citep{houghton2025roleabstractrepresentations, mccoy2023howmuchlanguage}.
Although \citet{mccoy2023howmuchlanguage} demonstrated that GPT-2 can
generate novel words and syntactic structures, the same study found that
it also reproduces a substantial portion of its training data verbatim.
Similarly, \citet{houghton2025roleabstractrepresentations} examined
binomial ordering preferences in eight models ranging from 124M to 70B
parameters and found no evidence of abstract preferences in any of them:
ordering preferences were driven entirely by relative frequency across
the board. Critically, a key ambiguity in this study is that the models
were trained on trillions of tokens, making it impossible to determine
whether the absence of abstract ordering preferences reflects a genuine
architectural limitation or simply the overwhelming influence of having
encountered each binomial many times over. Whether transformer models
can trade off between abstraction and storage in a human-like manner
therefore remains an open question.

A further complication is that examining models only at their final
training checkpoint may obscure important dynamics. If models initially
rely on abstract knowledge and gradually shift toward stored
representations as training progresses, end-of-training snapshots would
miss this trajectory entirely. Indeed,
\citet{houghton2025roleabstractrepresentations} explicitly flagged the
absence of such an analysis as a limitation, noting that their study was
not designed to investigate the effect of training data scale or how
ordering preferences develop across training. Examining learning curves
across checkpoints is therefore essential for understanding not only
whether human-like abstract-storage trade-offs emerge, but when and how
they develop.

\subsection{Present Study}\label{present-study}

To examine whether language models can trade off between abstract and
stored knowledge in a human-like manner, we extend
\citet{houghton2025roleabstractrepresentations} to models trained on a
developmentally plausible amount of data. Specifically, we train six
autoregressive language models from scratch using the OPT architecture
\citep{zhangOPTOpenPretrained2022}, crossing two training corpora
\citetext{\citealp[BabyLM,][]{charpentier2025babylmturns3}; \citealp[and
a subset of C4,][]{raffelExploringLimitsTransfer2020}} with three model
sizes (125M, 350M, and 1.3B parameters). Models are checkpointed
throughout training to track how binomial ordering preferences evolve as
a function of experience.

For each model and checkpoint, we computed the model's ordering
preference along with the model's preference deviation which captures
the degree to which the model's preferences deviate from what relative
frequency alone would predict. We then examine how these change as a
function of model's experience with binomials across training. By
examining ordering preferences across training, we assess not only
whether this human-like abstract-storage trade-off emerges, but when and
how it develops.

\section{Model Training}\label{model-training}

In order to examine the representation of binomials in language models,
we trained six autoregressive language models from scratch using the OPT
architecture \citep{zhangOPTOpenPretrained2022}, crossing two training
corpora with three model sizes (125M, 350M, and 1.3B parameters), for a
total of six models. All models share a common tokenizer: a Byte-Pair
Encoding (BPE) tokenizer with a vocabulary of 8,192 tokens and a context
window of 1,024 tokens, trained once on the full BabyLM corpus and
reused across all six models to ensure that differences between models
reflect training data and scale rather than tokenization. Models were
trained on two NVIDIA H100 GPUs using PyTorch Distributed Data Parallel
(DDP) in bfloat16 precision, with AdamW optimization and a linear
learning rate schedule with 4,000 warmup steps.

The first corpus that we trained the models on was BabyLM \citep[Version
3,][]{charpentier2025babylmturns3}, a developmentally plausible corpus
of approximately 150M tokens. Each model was trained for 64 epochs over
this corpus, yielding approximately 9.1B total training tokens. The
second corpus was a 10B-token subset of C4
\citep{raffelExploringLimitsTransfer2020}, a filtered English web text
corpus. C4 models were trained for a single pass over this corpus,
yielding approximately 10B training tokens. Comparing the two corpora
allows us to examine how the content and diversity of the training data
shapes the learning of binomials in language models.

Checkpoints were saved approximately every 10M tokens throughout
training and pushed to HuggingFace Hub, giving us dense coverage of each
model's learning trajectory. All models and checkpoints will be made
publicly available.\footnote{The huggingface account would reveal the
  identity of one of the authors and is thus omitted from the
  double-blind review phase.} Key training parameters for each model are
summarized in the Appendix (Table~\ref{tbl-models}).

All six models showed smooth convergence in training loss throughout
training, starting near the theoretical random baseline
(\(\ln 8192 \approx 9.01\)) and decreasing consistently. Perplexity on
the held-out validation set confirmed successful training for all
models; full loss curves and perplexity figures are reported in the
Appendix section~\ref{sec-training-quality}. Notably, the BabyLM 1.3B
model showed elevated final validation perplexity relative to the 350M
model (2,557 vs.~290), consistent with mild overfitting given the small
corpus (approximately 150M tokens) --- unsurprising for a 1.3B parameter
model trained on such limited data.

\section{Binomial Ordering
Preferences}\label{binomial-ordering-preferences}

\subsection{Dataset}\label{dataset}

The dataset used in all analyses is taken from
\citet{morganModelingIdiosyncraticPreferences2015}, which comprises 594
attested English binomial expressions annotated for various
phonological, semantic, and metrical constraints identified by
\citet{benorChickenEggProbabilistic2006}. For each binomial, the corpus
provides an abstract preference score (AbsPref), derived from a logistic
regression model combining these phonological, semantic, and metric
constraints \citep{morgan2016abstractknowledgedirect}. Abstract
Preferences capture the degree to which these constraints favor one
ordering over the other independently of corpus experience. This is the
same set of items used in
\citet{houghton2025roleabstractrepresentations}.

Frequency statistics for each binomial were estimated directly from the
two training corpora rather than from an external source. Crucially,
training from scratch with intermediate checkpoints allows us to compute
the exact counts for all binomial orderings at every stage of training,
for each model separately, rather than relying on approximations derived
from corpus statistics. For each binomial and checkpoint, we computed
the total number of exposures across both orderings. We eliminated
binomials that occurred 0 times in both orderings.\footnote{This yielded
  significantly fewer binomials in the BabyLM corpus due to it being a
  small corpus.} Note that this resulted in a different number of items
for the babyLM analyses vs the C4 analyses given the differences in
binomials present in each corpus. Table~\ref{tbl-corpus-stats}
summarizes the coverage of the attested binomials in each corpus.

\subsection{Analyses}\label{analyses}

In this section we present two complementary analyses examining how our
models represent binomial ordering preferences over the course of
training. The first examines model \textbf{preference} and
\textbf{preference deviation} at the end of training, asking what
factors predict each measure at the final checkpoint. The second
examines how these preferences develop over the course of training,
tracking how the influence of abstract and frequency-based factors
changes across checkpoints and how the overall magnitude of preferences
evolves as training progresses.

\subsubsection{Ordering Preferences at the Final
Checkpoint}\label{ordering-preferences-at-the-final-checkpoint}

We consider two complementary outcome measures. The first is model
\textbf{preference}: for each model, we computed the preference for a
given binomial by prepending each of 51 diverse prompt prefixes to both
orderings, computing the difference in summed token log-probability
between the alphabetically ordered form (e.g., \emph{bread and butter})
and the non-alphabetically ordered form (e.g., \emph{butter and bread})
within each prompt context, and averaging those per-prompt differences.
Positive values indicate a stronger preference for the alphabetically
ordered form (see Section~\ref{sec-def-preference} for a formal
definition). The second outcome is the \textbf{preference deviation}:
the difference between the model's preference score and the log relative
frequency ratio for that binomial (see Section~\ref{sec-def-prefdev} for
a formal definition). Because we train our models from scratch and
checkpoint them throughout training, we can compute the exact number of
times each model has seen each ordering of each binomial at every
checkpoint. A preference deviation of zero means the model's preference
exactly matches what relative frequency predicts; positive values
indicate a stronger preference for the alpha-ordered form than corpus
statistics alone would suggest, and negative values the reverse.

To help build an intuition for these measurements,
Figure~\ref{fig-scoring} illustrates both measures for five example
binomials from the C4 125M model at the final checkpoint. For example,
\emph{crannies and nooks} is strongly preferred in the non-alphabetical
ordering (i.e., \emph{nooks and crannies} is strongly preferred), while
\emph{bride and groom} is strongly preferred in the alphabetical
ordering. Moreover, \emph{nooks and crannies} is preferred even more
strongly than would be expected by the the model's training data, while
there is little difference between the expected preference and model's
preference for the binomial \emph{authority and force}. Interestingly,
Figure~\ref{fig-scoring} demonstrates that there can be quite large
difference between the training data and the model's preference. As a
case in point, while the expected preference of \emph{heir and son} and
\emph{crannies and nooks} is quite similar, the model learns a rather
extreme preference for the non-alphabetical ordering of \emph{crannies
and nooks}, but has very little preference for \emph{heir and son} in
either ordering (despite \emph{heir and son} occurring more than 300
times more in the nonalphabetical ordering (\emph{son and heir}) than in
the alphabetical ordering.

For each outcome, we implemented a Bayesian mixed-effects regression
model in R \citep{R-base} using the brms package
\citep{burknerBrmsPackageBayesian2017} with fixed effects for
\textbf{AbsPref} (Abstract Preference), \textbf{RelFreq}, and
\textbf{overall frequency}, interactions between each of AbsPref and
RelFreq with overall frequency, and random intercepts for
binomial.\footnote{For each statistical analysis, posterior predictive
  checks suggested adequate model fit. These are presented in the
  appendix, Section~\ref{sec-ppc}.} AbsPref captures the degree to which
abstract phonological and semantic constraints favor one ordering over
the other \citep{morgan2016abstractknowledgedirect}; RelFreq is the
proportion of corpus occurrences in the alphabetically ordered form,
centered (see Section~\ref{sec-def-relfreq}); and overall frequency is
the natural log of total occurrences in either ordering, logged and
centered. The interaction terms allow us to test whether the influence
of abstract constraints and relative frequency varies with overall
binomial frequency. All models used uninformative priors.

\begin{equation}\phantomsection\label{eq-main-model}{
\begin{aligned}
\{\text{preference},\ \text{pref. deviation}\} &\sim \text{AbsPref} \times \ln(\text{OverallFreq}) \\
  &\quad + \text{RelFreq} \times \ln(\text{OverallFreq}) + (1 \mid \text{binomial})
\end{aligned}
}\end{equation}

\paragraph{Results}\label{results}

\subparagraph{Preference}\label{preference}

The results of the Bayesian mixed-effects regression for preference at
the final checkpoint are visualized in Figure~\ref{fig-final-coefs}
(left; for full results, see Table~\ref{tbl-pref-final}, Appendix). The
results suggest that there is an effect of relative frequency on
ordering preferences across all six models, which is consistent with
models' ordering preferences being at least partly driven by the
distributional statistics of their training data. Interestingly, all
models except the C4 1.3B parameter model show an effect of AbsPref
(More than 89\% of posterior samples are greater than zero for the other
5 models). More specifically, the effect of AbsPref shows a graded
pattern across model sizes, with the 125M parameter models showing a
clear positive effect with the two smaller models for both datasets
showing a qualitatively (and in the case of the C4 1.3B model,
quantitatively) weaker effect.

\subparagraph{Preference Deviation}\label{preference-deviation}

The results of the Bayesian mixed-effects regression for preference
deviation at the final checkpoint are visualized in
Figure~\ref{fig-final-coefs} (right; for full results, see
Table~\ref{tbl-sg-final}, Appendix). The results for relative frequency
are quite mixed across model sizes and corpora. Among the BabyLM models,
the 350M model shows a negative effect of relative frequency, while the
two other models (125M and 1.3B) show little to no effect, suggesting
that the smaller model's ordering preferences are less extreme than
training would suggest (i.e., for binomials that occur more in one
ordering over the other, the model underestimates the strength of the
ordering preference expected by training data). Among the C4 models, the
pattern is slightly different: the two smaller models show a negative
effect of relative frequency, while the largest shows little evidence of
an effect, suggesting that the two smaller models similarly
underestimate the strength of the ordering preference. In terms of
abstract preferences, the 125M BabyLM model shows a weak positive effect
of AbsPref, while the two larger BabyLM models show no evidence of an
effect. For the C4 models, the pattern is similar: the C4 125M model
shows a weak effect of AbsPref (approximately 89\% of posterior samples
were greater than zero), while the two larger C4 models show little
evidence of an effect.

Taken together, the preference deviation results suggest that while
models' ordering preferences are broadly consistent with their training
data, the smaller models' preferences are also driven by abstract
preferences. These results suggest that smaller models may be more
sensitive to abstract ordering preferences even when controlling for
frequency.

\subsubsection{Development of Preferences Across
Training}\label{development-of-preferences-across-training}

To examine how ordering preferences develop over the course of training,
we fit the regression model in Equation~\ref{eq-main-model} separately
at 20 evenly-spaced checkpoints throughout training. Notably, the values
for overall frequency and relative frequency are calculated at the
respective checkpoint. We additionally examine how the overall magnitude
of preference deviations changes by computing the \textbf{mean absolute
preference deviation} across all binomials at each checkpoint, modeled
with a log-normal mixed-effects regression predicting absolute
preference deviation from training stage interacted with log overall
frequency (for full results, see Table~\ref{tbl-absgap-ln}, Appendix).

\paragraph{Preference and Preference
Deviation}\label{preference-and-preference-deviation}

AbsPref trajectories across training checkpoints are shown in
Figure~\ref{fig-absp-traj} (for trajectories of other coefficients, see
Appendix Section~\ref{sec-traj-all}). Both the smallest and largest
BabyLM models show an effect of abstract preference that emerges
mid-training and persists. Similarly, the smallest C4 model shows a
persistent AbsPref effect, while the larger C4 models show a U-shaped
trajectory: an effect emerges early but is subsequently reduced by
further training. The effect of abstract preference on preference
deviation is qualitatively similar, with the exception of the BabyLM
1.3B model, which shows no evidence of an AbsPref effect on deviation.
The absence of a U-shaped curve for the BabyLM models suggests that
training data diversity may also play a role in the learning of abstract
preferences.

\paragraph{Mean Absolute Preference
Deviation}\label{mean-absolute-preference-deviation}

Beyond the direction of preferences, we examined how the overall
magnitude of preference deviations changes over training. The mean
absolute preference deviation grows throughout training for all six
models (Figure~\ref{fig-corpus-tracking}, left), indicating that models'
preferences deviate increasingly from what relative frequency alone
would predict. Among the BabyLM models, this growth is most pronounced
for the 1.3B model and weakest for the 350M model. Among the C4 models,
all three follow a broadly similar trajectory.

The results of the Bayesian mixed-effects regression for absolute
preference deviation are visualized in Figure~\ref{fig-corpus-tracking}
(right; for full results, see Table~\ref{tbl-absgap-ln}, Appendix). The
effect of training stage is quite mixed across models. Among the BabyLM
models, the 125M and 1.3B models show a positive effect, while the 350M
model shows a negative effect. Among the C4 models, the two smaller
models show a slightly negative effect while the largest shows a
slightly positive effect. In general, the effect of checkpoint appears
weaker for the C4 models than for the BabyLM models, suggesting that
training data diversity may dampen the growth of preference magnitude
\citep[indeed, this is expected following evidence of adaptive partial
pooling in transformer
models,][]{kapatsinski2026transformersperformadaptive}.

The effect of overall frequency is much clearer. All six models show a
strong positive effect of overall frequency, indicating that the gap
between model preference and the frequency-based prediction is larger
for higher-frequency binomials. This pattern is of particular interest
given existing work demonstrating frequency-dependent preference
extremity in humans, whereby humans develop more extreme ordering
preferences for high-frequency binomials
\citep[e.g.,][]{morganFrequencydependentRegularizationIterated2016a, houghton2024frequencydependentpreferenceextremity, houghton2025boltsnutslanguage}.
The present results suggest that under certain circumstances language
models may show a similar pattern, though a full investigation of this
question is left for future research.

\subsection{Human Judgments}\label{sec-human}

To confirm that the ordering preferences learned by our models bear a
meaningful relationship to human behavior, we compared model preferences
to human ordering judgments collected for the same attested binomials.
Judgments were elicited using a two-alternative forced-choice (2AFC)
task: on each trial, participants were shown both orderings of a
binomial pair (e.g., ``bread and butter'' and ``butter and bread'') and
asked to select whichever felt more natural. Item order and choice
position (whether the binomial appeared on the left-side or right-side
of the screen) were randomized. 116 participants were recruited online
via Prolific.

At the final training checkpoint, model preferences correlated
positively with mean human preferences across all six models. Among the
BabyLM models, correlations were \(r = .59\) (125M), \(r = .45\) (350M),
and \(r = .47\) (1.3B). Among the C4 models, correlations were
\(r = .68\) (125M), \(r = .55\) (350M), and \(r = .66\) (1.3B). The full
trajectory of these correlations across training --- broken down by
overall frequency quintile --- is shown in
Figure~\ref{fig-human-model-cor} (top). For reference, the same figure
also shows the correlation between model preferences and relative
frequency across frequency quintiles (bottom), illustrating the degree
to which model preferences track corpus statistics. Together, these
results confirm that model preferences are meaningfully related to human
ordering judgments, providing a human baseline against which the
analyses below can be interpreted. For a more detailed exploration of
the human results, including supplementary statistical analyses, see
Appendix Section~\ref{sec-human-full}.

\section{Discussion and Conclusion}\label{discussion-and-conclusion}

The results of the present study suggest that the models trained here do
develop binomial ordering preferences over the course of training, and
that these preferences are at least partly driven by the distributional
statistics of the training data. Beyond frequency, however, the picture
is more nuanced. Smaller models (particularly the 125M models across
both corpora) show the most consistent evidence of AbsPref effects,
suggesting sensitivity to abstract ordering preferences over and above
what relative frequency alone would predict. Larger models show weaker
or absent AbsPref effects at the final checkpoint, though trajectory
results indicate sensitivity to AbsPref earlier in training, with the
effect weakening as training progresses --- a pattern more pronounced in
the C4 models, implicating data diversity. Smaller models, by contrast,
appear to maintain abstract preferences throughout training, perhaps
because they lack sufficient parameters to represent each binomial
distinctly. Together, these results suggest that the absence of abstract
preferences found in \citet{houghton2025roleabstractrepresentations} may
reflect training dynamics rather than an architectural limitation.
Finally, as training progresses, models' preferences grow more extreme
in a direction broadly consistent with relative frequency, but the
absolute gap between model preferences and frequency-based predictions
also grows --- modulated by model size and overall frequency --- in a
manner reminiscent of frequency-dependent preference extremity observed
in humans.

\section{The XOR Problem}\label{sec-xor}

Consider the XOR problem, in which a model must output 1 for inputs
(1,0) and (0,1), but 0 for inputs (0,0) and (1,1). Plotting these four
points in two-dimensional input space reveals the difficulty: the two
positive cases sit at diagonally opposite corners, with no straight line
that separates them from the two negative cases (Figure~\ref{fig-xor},
Panel A). A hidden layer solves this by mapping the four input points
into a new representational space defined by the hidden neurons'
activations. Each hidden neuron draws its own linear boundary through
the input space, assigning each input point a new position in a
coordinate space defined by those activation values. To see this
concretely, consider two hidden neurons applied to the XOR inputs. The
first neuron might learn to fire for any input where \(x_1 + x_2\)
\textgreater{} 0.5, drawing a diagonal boundary that separates (0,0)
from the other three points. The second might learn to fire only when
\(x_1 + x_2\) \textgreater{} 1.5, drawing a parallel boundary that
separates (1,1) from the other three. Each of the four input points now
has a pair of activation values, one from each hidden neuron, that
describes which side of each boundary it falls on. The input (0,0)
activates neither neuron; (1,0) and (0,1) activate the first but not the
second; and (1,1) activates both. These three distinct activation
signatures relocate the four points into a new space
(Figure~\ref{fig-xor}, Panel B) where the positive cases (1,0) and (0,1)
are cleanly separated from the negative cases by a single line, a
separation that was geometrically impossible in the original input
coordinates.

\begin{figure}

\centering{

\pandocbounded{\includegraphics[keepaspectratio]{writeup_files/figure-pdf/fig-xor-1.pdf}}

}

\caption{\label{fig-xor}The XOR problem illustrated. \textbf{A}: In the
original input space, no straight line separates the positive cases
(1,0) and (0,1) from the negative cases (0,0) and (1,1). \textbf{B}:
After applying two hidden neurons (one that fires when \(x_1 + x_2\)
\textgreater{} 0.5, another when \(x_1 + x_2\) \textgreater{} 1.5), the
four input points are relocated in hidden activation space. The two
positive inputs map to the same point (1, 0), and a single linear
boundary (dashed line) now correctly separates positive from negative
cases.}

\end{figure}%

\section{Mathematical Definitions}\label{sec-math-defs}

\subsection{Preference}\label{sec-def-preference}

For a binomial with alphabetically ordered token sequence \(s_\alpha\)
and non-alphabetically ordered sequence \(s_n\), and a set of \(K = 51\)
prompt prefixes \(\{c_k\}_{k=1}^{K}\), the model's \textbf{preference}
is:

\[\text{preference}_i = \frac{1}{K}\sum_{k=1}^{K}\Bigl[\log P_\theta(c_k \circ s_{\alpha,i}) - \log P_\theta(c_k \circ s_{n,i})\Bigr]\]

\[= \frac{1}{K}\sum_{k=1}^{K}\Bigl[\log P_\theta(s_{\alpha,i} \mid c_k) - \log P_\theta(s_{n,i} \mid c_k)\Bigr]\]

where \(\log P_\theta(s)\) is the summed token log-probability of
sequence \(s\) under next-token prediction with model parameters
\(\theta\), \(\circ\) denotes string concatenation, and the equality
follows from the chain rule of probability since \(\log P_\theta(c_k)\)
cancels across the difference.

\subsection{Preference Deviation}\label{sec-def-prefdev}

The \textbf{preference deviation} is the difference between a model's
preference and the log relative frequency ratio:

\[\text{preference deviation}_i = \text{preference}_i - \ln\frac{c_{\alpha,i}}{c_{n,i}}\]

where \(c_{\alpha,i}\) and \(c_{n,i}\) are the corpus counts of the
alphabetically ordered and non-alphabetically ordered forms of binomial
\(i\), respectively. A value of zero indicates that the model's
preference exactly matches what relative frequency predicts; positive
values indicate a stronger preference for the alpha-ordered form than
the corpus would suggest.

\subsection{Relative Frequency}\label{sec-def-relfreq}

\textbf{RelFreq} is the proportion of corpus occurrences in the
alphabetically ordered form, centered:

\[\text{RelFreq}_i = P(\text{alpha})_i - 0.5, \quad \text{where} \quad P(\text{alpha})_i = \frac{c_{\alpha,i}}{c_{\alpha,i} + c_{n,i}}\]

where \(c_{\alpha,i}\) and \(c_{n,i}\) are corpus counts of the
alpha-ordered and non-alphabetical forms of binomial \(i\).

\section{Human Judgment Results}\label{sec-human-full}

This appendix reports full results from the human judgment study
described in Section~\ref{sec-human}. We fit a Bayesian logistic
regression model using \texttt{brms} \citep{R-brms} predicting
participants' binary ordering responses (1 = \(\alpha\) ordering
chosen). The model used weakly informative priors (student\_t(3, 0, 0.1)
on the intercept; student\_t(3, 0, 0.05) on fixed effects and
random-effect standard deviations) and was run with 4 chains, 10,000
warmup iterations, and 10,000 sampling iterations.

The model examined the effects of abstract preference (AbsPref, centered
at 0) and relative frequency (RelFreq, centered at 0.5), each interacted
with log overall corpus frequency (OverallFreq, logged and centered).
Random slopes for all predictors were included by participant; random
intercepts were included by binomial item. The model equation is:

\[
\begin{aligned}
\text{resp} &\sim \text{AbsPref} * \text{OverallFreq} + \text{RelFreq} * \text{OverallFreq} \\
            &\quad + (\text{AbsPref} * \text{OverallFreq} + \text{RelFreq} * \text{OverallFreq} \mid \text{participant}) \\
            &\quad + (1 \mid \text{item})
\end{aligned}
\]
